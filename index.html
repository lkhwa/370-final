What makes a coffeehouse great? ☕ An analysis of user-submitted tips on Yelp.¶

By Leanne Hwa, Carol Cheng, Victoria Juan, and Kar Yin Ng¶

Project description 🗺️¶

On Yelp, where anyone can submit and read reviews, businesses can and should leverage the user-submitted data to not only better understand their customer feedback, but the greater competitive market.Some parts of the customer feedback are easily quantified, such as the number of stars out of 5 given, the useful, funny, or cool votes. However, what the user includes in the text of their feedback is even more important, containing raw insights and even complaints about their experience.

For this project, we wanted to focus on Yelp tips: shorter than reviews, but quick and straight to the point with suggestions. As characteristic of rating-oriented feedback, Yelp reviews are typically skewed towards the extreme ends of the 5-star spectrum, with users frequently leaving reviews only when they have an awesome or terrible experience. However, with tips not tied to a rating, we saw an opportunity for analyzing this more objective source of feedback.

The purpose of our research project is to better understand whats in a tip, and what its content may infer about a business overall rating. Weve scoped our project down to analyzing tips for coffee shops.

Our main research question is:

  • Is there a relationship between the attributes mentioned by users in tips and the business overall rating?

Our sub-questions are:

  • What are commonly mentioned business attributes in tips?
  • Does the context of an attribute in a tip vary across different business ratings?

Dataset description 📊¶

The dataset that we will be working with is a subset of Yelps businesses, reviews, and user data across 11 metropolitan areas in four countries. For this research study, we are specifically working with information about businesses (yelp_business.csv) and user-submitted tips (yelp_tip.csv). This dataset was originally collected for the Yelp Dataset Challenge where analyses and discoveries on Yelps data could be shared and made freely available. Methods 📓¶

What do users write about in tips?¶

To better understand the content of the tips in detail, from our top 100 words, we manually coded each word into four categories:

  1. Positive: conventionally positive words, such as adjectives (friendly), verbs (love), and features (free)
  2. Negative: conventionally negative words, such as adjectives (slow), verbs (wait)
  3. Neutral: business features or descriptors, not tied to a feeling, such as location
  4. Eliminated: stop-words (go, get), over-general words (food, coffee), or too niche words (chicken)

From this process, we were able to reduce our data size by 29%, with 29 words eliminated. Out of the remaining words, we had 21 positive, 5 negative, and 33 neutral, as seen below:

What are the most potentially impactful words mentioned in tips?¶

Our final words list is the 15 words that we felt had the most potential to have a relationship to the business overall rating, based on their flexibility (does the context of the word change across different tips?), scalability, and usefulness. Donuts were chosen just for fun because we all love a good donut!

5 words were chosen from each category: positive, negative, and neutral:

['love', 'friendly', 'free', 'fast', 'happy']
['wait', 'closed', 'busy', 'long', 'slow']
['service', 'atmosphere', 'location', 'wifi', 'donuts']

We then looked at one tip for each 0.5 rating increment that included each of our 15 words, to qualitatively analyze the context and sentiment of each word. From this data subset, we gathered a few interesting observations:

  • Positive words that were associated with feelings did change in context as the business rating increased; e.g. unfriendly (1.5) to friendly (2.0 and above); positive words could also be associated with attributes, such as free (free wifi) and happy (happy hour).
  • Negative words generally increased in positive sentiment as the business rating increased! For example, a 4.5 business tip used busy in the "usually never busy" context. With higher ratings, the negative word was usually nested in other positive compliments.
  • We found sentiments associated with neutral words as well - service and donuts changed from negative to positive sentiment as ratings increased; atmosphere and wifi were generally associated with a positive sentiment; location was generally associated with a negative sentiment.

Interpreting the results:¶

  • Looking at the coefficients, our results indicate both positive and negative relationships between words and the business rating:
    • Love, friendly, fast, happy, long, atmosphere, and donuts all have positive coefficients, which indicate that for a +1 increase in frequency of these words mentioned, there is an increase in the business' rating. This is consistent with our categorization of the words, with 4/5 positive words having this positive relationship. We hypothesize that long having this positive relationship can be correlated with how popular a business is - more visitors equals longer lines!
    • Free, wait, closed, busy, slow, service, location, and wifi all had negative coefficients, which indicate that for a +1 increase in frequency of these words mentioned, there is an decrease in the business' rating. This is also consistent with our categorizations - 4/5 negative words had this negative coefficient. We also hypothesized that customers are more likely to complain rather than compliment service and location.
  • Looking at our p-values, there is actually only one word that indicates strong evidence against the null hypothesis (no words have any impact on business rating): friendly!
    • The p-value for friendly is 0.001, so p < 0.05, and therefore we can reject the null hypothesis.
    • Friendly also has one of the highest positive coefficients, 0.0442.
  • The R^2 value of our model is 0.332. This is justified as tips are not connected to a users' star rating of the business, and an individual word can only represent a subset of the overall sentiment of the tip, as noted in our exploration of tips of different business ratings that included each word.

Conclusion 💭¶

Understanding customer feedback is critical to a business' success, and feedback comes in all forms, be it in-person conversations, emails, phone calls, or online. While Yelp reviews are a direct source of user feedback that allows users to rate a business and write in-depth about their experience, Yelp tips are the tl;dr, no-nonsense version of reviews that we were interested in exploring further.

We found that Yelp tips were a mix of recommendations for the business, and recommendations for other customers. By analyzing a variety of frequently mentioned words - positive words, negative words, and neutral features - in user-submitted tips, we were able to gain a better understanding of the relationship between these business attributes and user sentiments, and the business' overall rating.

Our final result pointed at mentions of friendly having the most positive impact on a business' rating, and a significant relationship. This is a critical finding that underscores the importance of good customer service on a business' success, and we hope our project can be helpful for businesses in better understanding their user feedback.
